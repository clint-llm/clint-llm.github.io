Toxoplasmosis is a common cause of central nervous system (CNS) infection in patients with HIV/AIDS who do not take appropriate prophylaxis. Cerebral toxoplasmosis typically manifests as one or more CNS mass lesions. Patients present with headache, confusion, and lethargy. This activity reviews the evaluation and treatment of toxoplasmosis and explains the important part played by the interprofessional team in improving care for patients with this condition.

**Objectives:**
- Describe the pathophysiology of toxoplasmosis.
- Review the use of pyrimethamine and sulphadiazine in the treatment of toxoplasmosis.
- Outline the criteria used in establishing a presumptive diagnosis of toxoplasmosis.
- Summarize the importance of collaboration and communication among the interprofessional team members to ensure compliance of patients with their HAART medications and the use of prophylaxis to avoid patients acquiring toxoplasmosis.
